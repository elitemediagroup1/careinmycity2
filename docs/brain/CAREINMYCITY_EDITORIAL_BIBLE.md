# CAREINMYCITY_EDITORIAL_BIBLE.md

**How All CareInMyCity Content Is Written**
*The editorial standard for every word on the site.*

> Inherits EMG_PLATFORM_BIBLE.md and CAREINMYCITY_BRAND_BIBLE.md.

---

## 1. Editorial Mission

Produce care content that is genuinely helpful, calmly reassuring, accurate, well-sourced, and never salesy — content a stressed family member can trust at 2 a.m.

## 2. Reader Profile

An overwhelmed adult child researching care for a parent, often under time pressure and emotional strain. They want clarity, honest information, concrete next steps, and trustworthy public resources — not a sales pitch and not a wall of jargon.

## 3. EEAT Standards

- **Experience:** write from genuine understanding of how care decisions actually unfold.
- **Expertise:** be accurate; verify facts; cite authoritative public sources.
- **Authoritativeness:** attribute content to a named author/organization with consistent schema.
- **Trustworthiness:** include visible disclaimers, honest framing, and transparent sourcing on every page.

## 4. Author Attribution Standards

- Every substantive page has a visible author or is attributed to the CareInMyCity editorial team / a named founder.
- Use consistent Person and Organization schema (see SEO Bible).
- Include a short, honest bio establishing relevant background.
- Show "reviewed/updated" dates where appropriate.

## 5. Medical / Legal / Financial Disclaimer Rules

- Every relevant page states clearly: educational information and public resources only; **not** medical, legal, financial, or insurance advice.
- Encourage consulting qualified professionals for specific situations.
- Never phrase guidance as a directive ("you should place your parent in...") — frame as "families often consider...".
- Disclaimers are visible and never removed.

## 6. Public-Resource Citation Standards

- Cite government and reputable nonprofit sources by name (Medicare, Medicaid, VA, Area Agencies on Aging, etc.).
- Link to the official source.
- Note that programs/eligibility vary and change; direct users to verify with the source.
- Never present public resources as proprietary or paywalled.

## 7. Tone Rules

Calm, clear, warm, practical, non-alarming, non-salesy, family-first. Acknowledge the emotional weight, then provide direction. Plain language; define any necessary term. Person-first language always ("your loved one", "your parent").

## 8. Formatting Rules

- Clear H1 (one per page), scannable H2/H3 structure.
- Short paragraphs; bullet lists only where they aid scanning.
- Bold sparingly for genuinely key points.
- Mobile-friendly line length and spacing.
- Descriptive links (no "click here").
- Front-load the answer; don't bury the help.

## 9. Service-Page Structure (REQUIRED)

Every service page should answer, in roughly this order:

1. **What the service is** — plain-language definition.
2. **Who it may help** — typical situations/needs.
3. **When families consider it** — common triggers/signs.
4. **Questions to ask** — a practical checklist for evaluating providers.
5. **Cost considerations** — general factors (no specific price promises).
6. **Medicare / Medicaid / VA considerations** — where appropriate, general guidance + "verify with the source".
7. **Public resources** — relevant government/nonprofit links.
8. **Documents to prepare** — what families should gather.
9. **How Carl can help** — pointer to the AI guide.
10. **How My Care Folder can help** — pointer to the org tool.
11. **Related services** — internal links to related service pages.

Plus: visible disclaimer + author attribution.

## 10. City-Page Structure

- Intro to care options in that city.
- Links to each available service in that city.
- Local/state public resources relevant to that area.
- Link up to the parent state page.
- How Carl + My Care Folder help locally.
- Disclaimer + attribution.

## 11. State-Page Structure

- Overview of care landscape and resources in that state.
- Statewide public resources (Medicaid program, AAA, etc.).
- Links to cities covered in that state.
- Links to services available statewide.
- Disclaimer + attribution.

## 12. FAQ Rules

- Answer real questions families actually ask.
- Concise, honest answers; link to deeper pages/resources.
- Use FAQ schema where appropriate (see SEO Bible).
- Never fabricate or pad with filler questions.

## 13. Local-Resource Section Rules

- Prioritize free, public, authoritative local resources.
- Keep current; flag that details may change and link to the official source.
- Make resources genuinely local — not the same national list pasted everywhere.

## 14. Caregiver-Friendly Language

- Compassionate, non-judgmental, person-first.
- Acknowledge stress and difficulty.
- Empower the reader with clear next steps.
- Avoid clinical detachment and avoid alarmism.

## 15. What to Avoid

- Medical/legal/financial/insurance advice or directives.
- Fear-selling, false urgency, false scarcity.
- Thin or duplicate (name-swap) content.
- Jargon without explanation.
- Overpromising ("guaranteed", "best", "cheapest").
- Cold or dehumanizing language ("the elderly", "the patient").
- Removing disclaimers or attribution.

## 16. Examples

**Good:** "Memory care is a type of senior living designed for people with Alzheimer's or other forms of dementia. Families often consider it when day-to-day safety becomes difficult to manage at home. Here are questions worth asking before you decide..."

**Bad:** "Your parent NEEDS memory care now — don't risk their safety. Get the best deal here!" *(fear-selling, advice-giving, salesy — never write this.)*

**Good (resource):** "Your state's Medicaid program may help cover some long-term care costs. Eligibility varies — check the official program page for current details."

**Bad (resource):** "We guarantee Medicaid will pay for everything." *(false, harmful — never write this.)*
